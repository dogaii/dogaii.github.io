My Hypothesis

My music listening duration and frequency vary according to daily weather conditions.

This bar chart represents the average monthly temperatures (°C) over one year, providing an overview of seasonal changes. I began the project by analyzing one year of weather data, which I fetched using historical weather APIs. This initial step helped establish the temperature patterns across seasons, forming a basis for comparing them with my Spotify listening habits.

Playtime gradually increases over the year, peaking in November and December. A noticeable dip occurs in September, potentially reflecting changes in routine, such as a return to work or school.

A general upward trend can be observed, suggesting increased listening activity over the year. Peaks in playback duration might align with specific events or personal routines, while dips could indicate less activity during certain periods.

This boxplot visualizes the distribution of daily playtime (hours) across months. The x-axis represents months, and the y-axis indicates daily playtime. Boxes represent interquartile ranges, while the whiskers and outliers show variability. Playtime exhibits seasonal trends, with higher activity from September to December. June shows the highest variability, possibly due to varying routines like vacations or holidays. The overall median playtime increases slightly as the year progresses.

This heatmap represents average playtime (in hours) for each month. The color intensity ranges from light to dark, with darker shades indicating higher playtime. Peak listening activity is visible in the later months of the year (October to December). Mid-year months (June to August) also show increased playtime compared to earlier months.

The plot uses varying shades of blue to indicate the density of data points: Darker Areas: Higher concentration of data points. Lighter Areas: Lower concentration of data points. At very low temperatures (below 5°C) and very high temperatures (above 25°C), the density fades, indicating less playback duration on those days. This aligns with the observation that extreme weather may reduce my music listening, possibly due to changes in activity levels or comfort.

A slight positive trend can be observed: as temperature increases, average playtime also tends to rise. Sunny and Hot conditions dominate higher temperature ranges, while Rainy and Cold conditions cluster in lower temperature ranges. The diversity of weather conditions provides a nuanced view of how listening habits adapt to different temperatures.

The hexagons represent the density of data points: Color Gradient: The darker (red) hexagons indicate areas with higher concentrations of data points (higher point density), while lighter hexagons (blue) represent lower density. Used the hexbin function in Matplotlib to create a hexagonal binning plot. The color bar on the right represents the point density within each hexagon. High Density Around Mild Temperatures: The darkest (red) areas occur around temperatures between 10°C and 20°C, with playtime between 30–50 hours. This suggests that I listen to the most music during mild weather conditions. Reduced Playtime at Extreme Temperatures: Fewer data points (lighter hexagons) are observed at very low (< 5°C) or very high (> 25°C) temperatures, indicating that I listen to less music in extreme weather.
